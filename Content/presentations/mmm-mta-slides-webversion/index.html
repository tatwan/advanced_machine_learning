Multi-Touch Attribution
(MTA)

What is Multi-Touch Attribution (MTA)?

Marketing measurement method that assigns credit for conversions across ALL touchpoints in the customer journey

  • Key Concept: Distributes credit rather than giving 100% to one interaction
  • Focus: User-level, digital journey tracking
  • Granularity: Individual campaign and ad performance
  • Data Source: User tracking (cookies, device IDs, pixels)

MTA Attribution Models: Rule-Based

Linear

Credit: Equal credit to all touchpoints

Best For: Long consideration cycles, awareness campaigns

Example: 4 touchpoints = 25% each

25% | 25% | 25% | 25%

Time Decay

Credit: More credit to recent touchpoints

Best For: Short sales cycles, promotional campaigns

Assumption: Recency = Influence

10% | 20% | 30% | 40%

U-Shaped (Position-Based)

Credit: 40% first, 40% last, 20% middle

Best For: Awareness + conversion focus

Philosophy: Values discovery and conversion equally

40% | 10% | 10% | 40%

W-Shaped

Credit: First, key middle, and last touchpoints

Best For: Complex B2B journeys with nurturing

Philosophy: Recognition of key conversion moments

30% | 10% | 30% | 30%

MTA Attribution Models: Data-Driven

Algorithmic / Data-Driven Attribution

Uses machine learning and historical data to learn optimal credit distribution

Common Approaches

  • Markov Chains: Evaluate "removal effect"‚Äîwhat happens if touchpoint is removed?
  • Shapley Value: Game theory approach for fair credit allocation
  • Logistic Regression: Predict conversion probability per touchpoint

Advantages

  • Learns from actual conversion patterns
  • Adapts to your specific customer journey
  • More accurate than rule-based models

Challenges

  • Requires significant historical data
  • Needs ML expertise to implement
  • Less transparent than rule-based

Example: Markov Chain Attribution

Calculates the probability of conversion with and without each touchpoint, measuring incremental contribution

Marketing Mix Modeling
(MMM)

What is Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM)?

Statistical analysis using aggregated data to measure the impact of marketing activities on sales

  • Historical Technique: Developed in 1960s-70s, recently resurged due to privacy changes
  • Focus: Channel-level, strategic budget decisions
  • Key Question: "How much does each channel contribute to overall sales?"
  • Comprehensive: Analyzes both online AND offline channels (TV, radio, print, digital)
  • Privacy-Friendly: Uses aggregated data, no individual user tracking required

How MMM Works: The Equation

Sales = Base Sales + Marketing Effect + External Factors + Error

üìä Base Sales

Natural demand without marketing

  • Price effects
  • Seasonality patterns
  • Long-term trends
  • Brand equity

üì¢ Marketing Effect

Incremental sales from advertising

  • TV spend
  • Digital campaigns
  • Radio advertising
  • Print media

üåç External Factors

Outside influences on sales

  • Economic indicators
  • Weather conditions
  • Competitor actions
  • Special events

Statistical Approach

MMM uses multiple regression analysis with time-series data (typically weekly or monthly) to isolate the impact of each marketing channel while controlling for all other factors.

MMM Key Concepts: Adstock

The Problem

Advertising effects don't happen instantly‚Äîthey carry over time

What is Adstock?

Adstock models the lagging, decaying effect of advertising over time

  • Example: TV ad runs in Week 1, but people buy in Weeks 2, 3, and 4
  • Carryover Effect: Ad impressions don't disappear immediately from memory
  • Different by Channel: TV has longer adstock than search ads
Adstockt = Spendt + Œ± √ó Adstockt-1

Parameter Œ± (alpha): Decay rate (0-1)

  • Œ± = 0: No carryover (immediate effect only)
  • Œ± = 0.5: 50% of previous week's effect carries over
  • Œ± = 0.9: Strong carryover (long-lasting brand campaigns)

MMM Key Concepts: Saturation

The Problem

More spend doesn't always equal proportionally more sales

Diminishing Returns

  • Initial Spend: First $10K reaches eager, high-intent customers ‚Üí High ROI
  • Additional Spend: Next $10K reaches less interested audience ‚Üí Lower ROI
  • Saturation Point: Eventually, additional spend yields minimal incremental sales
  • Non-Linear Effect: Relationship between spend and sales follows an S-curve

Saturation Functions

Common mathematical functions used to model saturation:

  • Logistic Function: Classic S-curve shape
  • Hill Function: Used in pharmacology, flexible saturation modeling
  • Michaelis-Menten: From enzyme kinetics, models saturation well

When to Use MMM vs MTA

Use MMM When:

  • Need to measure offline channels (TV, radio, print, out-of-home)
  • Making strategic, long-term budget allocation decisions
  • Privacy regulations limit user tracking capabilities
  • Want to include external factors (economy, seasonality, weather)
  • Have months-years of historical data available
  • Need holistic view of entire marketing mix

Use MTA When:

  • Focus on digital-only campaigns and channels
  • Need granular, campaign-level performance insights
  • Optimizing short-term tactical decisions
  • Can track user-level data compliantly
  • Have complex digital customer journeys to analyze
  • Want real-time or near-real-time attribution

Best Approach: Use BOTH

Unified Measurement: Combine MMM for strategic planning with MTA for tactical optimization

MMM provides the strategic roadmap, MTA provides the tactical execution details

MTA Implementation Considerations

Data Requirements

  • User-Level Tracking: Cookies, device IDs, authenticated user data
  • CRM Integration: Connect touchpoints to actual conversions and revenue
  • Cross-Platform Data: Web analytics, mobile app, email, social platforms
  • Data Quality: Clean, consistent tracking across all touchpoints

Technical Setup

  • Attribution Platform: Google Analytics 4, Adobe Analytics, or custom solution
  • Tag Management: Google Tag Manager for tracking implementation
  • Data Warehousing: BigQuery, Snowflake, or similar for data aggregation
  • Identity Resolution: Cross-device and cross-session user matching

Key Challenges

  • Data Quality: Ensuring complete and accurate tracking across channels
  • Identity Resolution: Matching users across devices and sessions
  • Privacy Compliance: GDPR, CCPA consent management
  • Attribution Window: Determining appropriate lookback period
  • Walled Gardens: Limited data sharing from platforms like Facebook, Google

Practical Application: MMM Case Study

Consumer Packaged Goods (CPG) Company

Scenario: Large CPG company with $50M annual marketing budget

Channels: TV, Radio, Print, Digital Display, Search, Social Media

Goal: Optimize budget allocation across all channels

MMM Findings:

  • TV Advertising: 35% contribution to sales | Highest ROI during Q4 holiday season
  • Digital Search: 25% contribution | Consistent performance year-round
  • Social Media: 15% contribution | Reached saturation point, diminishing returns
  • Radio: 10% contribution | Regional effectiveness varies significantly
  • Print: 8% contribution | Long adstock effect but declining effectiveness
  • Display: 7% contribution | Strong support channel for other media

Action Taken

  • Reallocate 20% budget from social to TV during Q4
  • Reduce print spend by 30%
  • Increase search budget by 15%
  • Optimize radio to high-performing regions

Results

  • 12% increase in total sales
  • 15% better ROI overall
  • $6M additional revenue with same budget
  • More efficient media mix

Combining MMM & MTA: Unified Measurement

Why Combine Both?

MMM Strengths:

  • Strategic view
  • Offline + online channels
  • Privacy-safe
  • External factors included

MTA Strengths:

  • Tactical granularity
  • Real-time insights
  • Campaign-level detail
  • Journey understanding

Unified Measurement Approach

  1. Use MMM for strategic budget allocation across all channels (online + offline)
  2. Use MTA for tactical optimization of digital campaigns within the allocated budget
  3. Cross-validate insights between both methodologies to identify discrepancies
  4. Triangulate with incrementality tests (A/B tests, geo-lift experiments)

The Measurement Triangle

Result: More robust, complete picture of marketing effectiveness
